OUTREACH CHEAT SHEET

Starting the conversation

The goal of the first chat isn't to pitch — it's to get them talking about where they're losing time, quality, or money. Lead with a symptom, then ask. You're listening for a “yeah, that's us.”

Does any of this sound familiar?

Drop one or two of these into a message or the opening of a call. You only need one to land.

  • Your operators dial in machine settings from experience — and when a run goes bad, there's no record of what changed.
  • If a customer flags a defect, you can't easily trace that part back to the run that made it.
  • You're half-guessing at what's actually on the floor right now — WIP and finished goods.
  • When a machine goes down, nobody really captures why — so the true cost of downtime is a mystery.
  • Scrap and rework quietly eat into material cost, but no one could tell you what it adds up to in a year.

Questions to keep it going

Open-ended, not leading. These make you sound like someone who's been in a plant — not a software vendor.

Quality & Scrap

  • When a bad batch happens, how do you figure out why?
  • What share of what you run ends up as scrap or rework?
  • Could anyone here tell me what scrap costs you in a year?

Traceability

  • If a customer returns a defect, can you trace it to the run that made it?
  • How long would that take you today?

Inventory

  • How do you know what's on the floor right now?
  • How often is the system wrong vs. what's actually there?

Capacity & Downtime

  • Where do you think you're losing the most capacity?
  • Do you know your OEE — or how much more you could ship with what you already have?

What's running the floor

  • What runs the floor today — paper, spreadsheets, an ERP, tribal knowledge?
  • What's the one thing you wish you could see that you can't?

The priority

  • If we fixed just one of these next quarter, which would matter most?
  • What's stopped you from tackling it so far?
How to close

No pitch. End with: “Let me send you a short note — what I heard, and the one thing I'd look at first.” Then deliver that half-page the same day. The recap is your real first product: fast, free, and specific.
